1. Abstract

Cross-speaker emotion transfer speech synthesis aims to synthesize emotional speech for a target speaker by transferring the emotion from reference recorded by another (source) speaker, where extracting speaker-independent emotion embedding from reference plays an important role. However, the emotional information of such emotion embedding tends to be weakened in the process to squeeze out the source timbre information. In this paper, a prosody compensation module (PCM) is proposed to compensate emotional information for the disentangled emotion embedding. Specifically, the PCM tries to obtain speaker-independent emotional information \textcolor{red}{from the bottleneck feature of a pre-trained ASR model}. To this end, a prosody compensation encoder with GC blocks is introduced to obtain global emotional information from the ASR model's bottleneck feature. Experiments demonstrate that the proposed PCM can effectively compensate emotional information to the emotion embedding, and meanwhile maintain the timbre of the target speaker. Comparisons with state-of-the-art models show that our proposed method presents obvious superiority on the cross-speaker emotion transfer task.



The emotion is transferred from emotional speaker AIC and DB_6 randomly. Among them, the obvious timbre difference between the little girl (the source speaker AIC) and the adult female (the target speaker DB_1) makes it easier for the listener to perceive the timbre. Therefore, we first provide samples of each experiment with AIC as the emotional reference for transferring the emotion to the target speaker DB_1, and samples with DB_6 as the emotional reference will also be provided in the following.

4. Demos -- The effectiveness of GC block

As we can see that in terms of the emotion similarity, CSPC significantly outperforms the w/o GC without the GC block.

As we can see that in terms of the speaker similarity, dropping the GC block brings a slight increase which is not significant.
